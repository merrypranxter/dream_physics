Rare or Anomalous Dream Types 

-  Lucid Dreaming (Dream Control): A lucid dream is one in which you become aware that
you’re dreaming while still inside the dream. This often allows a degree of control over 
the dream events. The experience is like “waking up” within the dream: the dreamer 
realizes nothing is real and can consciously choose actions. Some use this to fly, 
perform superhuman feats, or alter the environment at will. In a lucid state, you might 
literally decide to rewrite the dream’s script – for example, instantly transporting yourself 
to a new scene or summoning a character. It’s as if you’re the director of your own movie 
. Lucid dreams are relatively uncommon but well-documented, and they are usually very 

vivid and memorable because of the dreamer’s active role. 

-  False Awakening: This phenomenon entails dreaming of waking up. You believe you

have come out of sleep, and you often go through ordinary motions (getting out of bed, 
getting dressed, starting your routine) all within the dream. A classic example is 
dreaming that you shut off your alarm and begin your morning, only to abruptly truly 
wake up and realize none of it happened. False awakenings can sometimes layer – e.g. 
you “wake up” from a dream, tell someone about it, then wake up again for real. During a 
false awakening, the bedroom or environment often looks eerily realistic, making it hard 
to immediately recognize it as a dream. The telltale sign is the shock or disorientation 
when you finally wake up for real, realizing that an entire episode of seemingly being 
awake was in fact part of the dream . 

-  Sleep Paralysis Hallucination: Sleep paralysis occurs when your mind awakens from

REM sleep but your body is still immobilized (a natural REM state). In this half-dreaming 
state, people often experience terrifying hallucinations. Typically, the dreamer wakes up 
in their bedroom, unable to move or cry out, and feels a malevolent presence in the 
room. Commonly, sufferers report seeing or sensing an entity – a shadowy figure, a 
demon sitting on their chest, an intruder – or feeling pressure on their body. The 
emotional impact is extreme fear, as one is conscious but completely paralyzed. These 
visions are essentially a blending of dream imagery with waking reality . Sleep paralysis 
episodes are short (seconds to minutes) but very frightening; they often end with the 
person fully awakening, heart racing. (Though not a typical “dream” since it happens at 
wake-sleep boundary, it’s a notable anomalous experience related to dreaming.) 

-  Shared (Mutual) Dreams: This is a controversial and anecdotal phenomenon where two
or more people report having the same dream content. For example, close friends or 
twins might wake up and discover they both dreamed of meeting each other in the same 
place, with matching details. Scientifically, no controlled proof exists, but there are 
thousands of well-documented cases in literature and reports . The most credible involve 
people with close emotional bonds (e.g. a therapist and client, or siblings) independently 
describing an identical dream. In such a shared dream, both individuals experience a 
similar scenario or even interact with each other within the dream. While explanations 
are elusive, the raw experience reported is that “we dreamed the same thing.” These 
cases are rare and often only discovered when one person mentions the dream and the 
other gasps in recognition. 

-  Precognitive (Premonition) Dreams: On occasion, a person will dream of an event before
it happens in real life. In a precognitive dream, the content seemingly foreshadows future 
occurrences with striking accuracy – for instance, dreaming of a specific accident, 
conversation, or scenario, and then encountering that exact situation later when awake. 
When the real event unfolds, the dreamer experiences intense déjà vu, recognizing it 
from their dream. Historically and anecdotally, many people have reported such 
experiences (e.g. dreaming of a distant relative’s death before receiving news of it). 

Scientifically, these could be coincidences or memory tricks, but the subjective 
phenomenon is that the dream felt like a preview of the future. Precognitive dreams are 
very rare and unpredictable, but they have been a source of fascination in both folklore 
and modern research. 

-  Recurring Dreams: A recurring dream is any dream scenario or theme that repeats over

multiple nights or years. The individual experiences a very similar dream on many 
separate occasions, sometimes over a long span of time. The content can vary widely 
(being in the same strange house, repeatedly encountering a certain person or conflict, 
etc.), but it’s recognizably the same dream again. Often these dreams are unwanted or 
troubling, though not always nightmares. The hallmark is the familiarity – during the 
dream you may even think, “I’ve been here before.” Recurring dreams often feature 
unresolved situations that come back again and again. For example, a person might 
have the nightmare of being back in school unprepared for a test dozens of times across 
their life . Or one might revisit the same imaginary location frequently. The raw 
experience is a feeling of deja vu within the dream every time it happens. 

-  Visitation Dreams: In these dreams, a deceased person (often a close loved one)

appears in a very vivid, realistic manner. The departed individual may look healthy and 
real, and they often communicate or convey a message to the dreamer. Visitation 
dreams stand out for their clarity and the powerful emotions involved – the dreamer 
typically feels the presence of the lost person as if it were actual reality . There may be 
hugging, conversation, or simply a meaningful glance, but it feels profoundly significant. 
Many report a strong sense that “it was really them,” beyond an ordinary dream. These 
experiences are raw and personal (regardless of one’s beliefs about afterlife); the 
common factor is the feeling of a real visit. Upon waking, the dreamer often feels 
comforted, shocked, or emotionally moved because the interaction felt authentic. 

-  Night Terrors (Sleep Terrors): Night terrors are very intense episodes of fear that occur

during deep non-REM sleep, most often in children. Unlike typical nightmares (which are 
dreams with scary content), a night terror is more of a panic attack during sleep. The 
person (usually a child) will suddenly scream, cry, or show extreme terror while still fully 
asleep, often with eyes open and a racing heart. They might thrash around as if fighting 
or fleeing. Crucially, if they wake up, they usually do not remember any dream or 
imagery of what scared them . The experience is “raw terror” without a story – the child 
might recall only a feeling or a vague image, or nothing at all. From the outside, it’s 
alarming: the sleeper appears petrified and inconsolable for a few minutes, then calms 
down and has no memory of it in the morning. Night terrors are relatively rare and mostly 
outgrown with age, but they represent an anomalous kind of dream phenomenon where 
emotion (fear) occurs without any coherent dream narrative. 

Each of these phenomena, from the everyday quirks to the rare extremes, represents the kinds 
of raw experiences people report from their dream life. This taxonomy spans the gamut from 

common distortions (like fuzzy vision or time dilation in dreams) to extraordinary occurrences
